# Proposition to build a guild of software developers

Briefly, I would like to see the wide population of developers and developer-adjacent professionals
get organized into an industry-wide guild.

I envision a hiring hall, a continuing education and training program, a professional code of conduct,
an apprenticeship program, and pipelines for both journeyman and senior developers.

## Summary Up Front

Let's reframe software development (and tech work generally) as a true professional discipline rather
than a loosely regulated labor market driven primarily by corporate hiring needs.
Tech workers lack the structural safeguards, ethical standards, apprenticeship pathways,
and collective institutions that exist in other engineering professions,
leaving workers vulnerable to exploitative hiring practices, unethical corporate directives,
unstable employment cycles, and fragmented career development.

The Guild concept combines elements of medieval guilds, labor organizing, workforce development,
and certification systems into a worker-centered ecosystem built around shared ethical standards,
mentorship, peer accountability, and collective advancement through

* a code of professional conduct,
* community-recognized certifications tied to demonstrated skill and experience,
* mentorship and apprenticeship structures connecting senior and junior workers,
* and eventually a hiring-network model that prioritizes worker development and long-term professional sustainability over purely profit-driven incentives.

The broader theory is that by creating institutions capable of coordinating workers across the industry,
rather than leaving individuals to negotiate alone within asymmetrical labor markets,
the profession could gradually establish stronger bargaining power, improve hiring quality and trust,
reduce exploitative dynamics, and create more stable pathways for technical workers across industries.

## Code of Professional Conduct

A code of conduct can become a foundation for respect for an organization.
When members agree to the code, they are voluntarily giving up certain freedoms
in order to solve a collective-action problem. With any luck, workers will
recognize that problem for what it is: The *race-to-the-bottom* dynamic
in terms of developer ethics. "I won't do X, but someone will, so I may as well be one to get paid."
If we know that *indeed* (almost) nobody will do X, then X becomes too expensive.

Consider, for example, the Volkswagen emissions cheating scandal.
Some executive decided to cheat the law, but some development team actually wrote the code.
At some point, someone *both* knew the purpose *and* translated that purpose into code.
Whoever did that is, in my book, guilty of conduct unbecoming of a professional and
should be drummed out. But how do we generalize such a respect for:

* Ethics
* Safety
* Fitness for Purpose
* Legal Compliance
* Environmental Impact
* Bystander Impact
* Advancement of the Profession

The ACM has a code of conduct which strikes a pretty good balance.
I don't know if we adopt this wholesale, or modify it, but we should consider it.

I'd ask that members of the guild would subscribe (perhaps with an oath) to whatever professional code of conduct,
and also that there be a working group composed of interested members who periodically update the thing to address
recent complications and quandries as they arise.

## Developer Quality Certification Program

Suppose we can track members' experience, skills, and aptitudes.
We can direct continuing education, topical training, and the like.
With apprenticeship and journeyman dev programs, we can see to a nice
progression of professional growth with time - or help people see the
error of sticking with unhappy ways.

## Hiring Hall

With a respected *seal-of-approval* for dev qualifications,
we can become a trusted source for trustworthy devs.

In the beginning, this can function similar to a member-owned and operated recruiting firm.
You put your name on a list, and meanwhile we track you experience and qualifications.

As someone comes forth and needs, say, 10 junior devs, we just file names off the top of the list and boom.
Or if 10 people are laid off, they go to the bottom of the list for their particular experience level.

The ultimate goal is to kill off Linked-In, Indeed, Dice, Monster, and all the other for-profit technical recruiting sites,
at least in regard to the software development profession.

## Apprenticeship Program

Align this with typical engineering professional programs for e.g. mechanical, process, civil, etc.
