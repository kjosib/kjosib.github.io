# Effortless Performance

A well-trained voice requires no effort, imposes neither difficulty nor strain, neither physical nor mental nor emotional.

* You do not need to strap weights to your vocal muscles.
* Flexibility, habit, coordination, and endurance each are another question.

Under ordinary circumstances, people tend to have sufficient musculature through normal use.
There are special cases. Don't feel bad if you are one, but it's uncommon.

If you typically speak less than 30 minutes in a day, or only very softly, you may need to work up some general vocal endurance to avoid overuse injuries.

* Let me know if this applies to you!

# Does it feel completely weird?

You're doing something completely new. It's going to feel strange and unfamiliar the first few times.
This is a good sign that you're learning. Neuroplasticity is a wonderful thing!

# About Entrained Tension

During the process of exploring and refining the voice, you'll be developing new coordination and habits with muscles you already use daily.

As your brain is learning how to walk and chew gum at the same time (so to speak) it's common for unrelated motor nerves to also fire,
contracting other muscles beside what we need. This is a fairly normal part of exploration and it works itself out quickly as you hone in on the skill.
Think back to when you learned to balance and steer a bicycle (or write): At first you might have been wobbly (or held the pencil with a death-grip)
but soon enough you relaxed everything out and applied just minimal corrective forces. The activity became effortless.

We want to train for that *minimal-effort behavior* as quickly as possible!

Sometimes, people accidentally entrain needless extra muscular behavior *as part of the motor skill.* 
This can manifest in soreness, tightness, tiredness, inflamation, or the like.
If you notice something of this nature, ***please tell me promptly!***

The sooner we decouple those other behaviors from our desired modifications to the *sound* of the voice, the better!

# About Fatigue

**Early in training:**

When you're first starting out, you're going to be using your vocal tract anatomy in ways it's not used to.
Although you (probably) have the *strength and power* for short bursts of this activity,
it may take time to work up the *endurance* to hold new postures as you learn the coordination.

To mitigate this risk, the general principles are spaced repetition and brief practice sessions.
These might last as little as 30 to 90 seconds each, maybe 5 to 10 times a day at odd intervals.
This is not a physical work-out; it's a reminder for your brain that it's important to retain a skill
learned during coaching. Short sessions minimize the risk of fatigue and help keep you from getting bored.

To be clear, there is flexibility here. Different people's brains (and lives) work differently.
If you'd rather do a couple of 15 minute session daily, that's your choice.
A good schedule is one you can acheive. Just don't expect great results from infrequent long cram sessions:
The neuroscience on physical learning suggests it's generally hard to retain new skills that way.
Plus, it does significantly increase the risk of fatigue, which leads to poor form.

In a perfect world, a practice session should be no more strenuous than eating a light meal while
holding a conversation normally. If it feels like things are getting much more difficult than that,
take a day of vocal rest and see if things improve thereafter. If not, please do speak up and let me know!
I'd like to try to figure out the problem and help fix it. I'll make time for you.

**Later in training:**

As people begin to find their new voices, there's sometimes a tendency to vastly increase the amount of
speaking they do. Unsurprisingly, this can lead to overuse injury. *Please* listen to your body!
Occasional vocal-rest days are a valuable part of a healthy program. You can absolutely work up to
full-time trans-voice, even giving lectures or one-person shows, but it takes time and care to build this facility.

An increase of 10% per week in time spent using the voice is a fine starter guideline.
See how you feel and adjust from there, but not in major increments.
