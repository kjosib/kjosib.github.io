# Effortless Performance

A well-trained voice requires no effort, imposes neither difficulty nor strain, neither physical nor mental nor emotional.

* You do not need to strap weights to your vocal muscles.
* Flexibility, habit, coordination, and endurance each are another question.

Under ordinary circumstances, people tend to have sufficient musculature through normal use.
There are special cases. Don't feel bad if you are one, but it's uncommon.

If you typically speak less than 30 minutes in a day, or only very softly, you may need to work up some general vocal endurance to avoid overuse injuries.

* Let me know if this applies to you!

# Does it feel completely weird?

You're doing something completely new. It's going to feel strange and unfamiliar the first few times.
This is a good sign that you're learning. Neuroplasticity is a wonderful thing!

# About Entrained Tension

During the process of exploring and refining the voice, you'll be developing new coordination and habits with muscles you already use daily.

As your brain is learning how to walk and chew gum at the same time (so to speak) it's common for unrelated motor nerves to also fire,
contracting other muscles beside what we need. This is a fairly normal part of exploration and it works itself out quickly as you hone in on the skill.
Think back to when you learned to balance and steer a bicycle (or write): At first you might have been wobbly (or held the pencil with a death-grip)
but soon enough you relaxed everything out and applied just minimal corrective forces. The activity became effortless.

We want to train for that *minimal-effort behavior* as quickly as possible!

Sometimes, people accidentally entrain needless extra muscular behavior *as part of the motor skill.* 
This can manifest in soreness, tightness, tiredness, inflamation, or the like.
If you notice something of this nature, ***please tell me promptly!***

The sooner we decouple those other behaviors from our desired modifications to the *sound* of the voice, the better!
